Information Lattice Learning as Probabilistic Graphical Model Structure Learning

Information Lattice Learning (ILL) interprets a signal by projecting it onto a partition lattice encoding abstraction hierarchies, then lifting learned rules back to the original domain. When applied to probability mass functions, the probabilistic rules derived from ILL naturally correspond to constraints in a Probabilistic Graphical Model (PGM). These rule sets represent marginal constraints over interpretable abstractions defined by the lattice's partitions. The feasible family of joint distributions satisfying these constraints is addressed via "general lifting," often implemented using an L2 uniformity principle related to maximum entropy. Therefore, ILL is best viewed as structure learning for constraint-based factor graphs built upon quotient variables, rather than a standard Bayesian network.

A study examines the evolution of the Weibull weight-scale parameter ($\lambda$) during AdamW training using a two-parameter Weibull framework. The research decomposes the squared weight norm's change into three forces: alignment, injection, and decay. On Pythia-70M models, the alignment force dominates the initial rise phase, contributing 88-94% of the total absolute force budget. At near saturation, the balance between alignment and decay explains the transition to parameter relaxation. The authors propose a spline displacement method to estimate the crucial alignment force even when optimizer moments are unavailable.

Zero-Inflated Gaussian Distributions Enable Parameter-Space Sparsity in Estimation-of-Distribution Algorithms

Estimation-of-distribution algorithms (EDAs) typically struggle when applied to sparse parameter spaces because existing methods require handcrafted sparsity operators. The authors propose using multivariate zero-inflated Gaussian (ZIG) distributions as the sampling law for EDAs, enabling optimization in sparse settings without such restrictive assumptions. ZIG models account for both sparsity patterns and active parameter values simultaneously through a latent Gaussian structure. They introduce practical amortized inversion-based estimators that accurately recover these latent correlation structures. Testing on the Lunar Lander benchmark showed that ZIG-EDA converges faster and achieves superior results compared to dense or existing sparse evolutionary algorithms.

ProMUSE: Progressive Multi-modal Uncertainty-guided Staged Evidential Alzheimer Disease Classification

Alzheimer's disease diagnosis increasingly relies on expensive multimodal data like structural MRI and PET scans. ProMUSE introduces a Progressive Multi-modal Uncertainty Guided Staged Evidential Network to address the impracticality of full-modality inference in clinics. Initially, it classifies using low-cost clinical data while quantifying uncertainty with a Dirichlet-based model. If uncertainty is high, ProMUSE adaptively incorporates MRI or PET features, fusing beliefs via Dempster-Shafer theory for a calibrated prediction. Testing across multiple AD datasets confirmed ProMUSE achieves competitive accuracy while significantly reducing the need for costly imaging by 50-90%.

cAPM: Continual AI-Assisted Pace-Mapping with Active Learning

Ventricular tachycardia ablation requires precise pace-mapping, which is manually intensive and time-consuming. Existing AI methods for guidance fail to transfer knowledge across different ventricular tachycardia (VT) targets within the same patient. cAPM introduces a novel framework employing continual learning and a task-agnostic surrogate neural network to accumulate and utilize data from previous mapping sessions. This allows it to efficiently guide clinicians by identifying the most informative pacing sites sequentially. In simulations, cAPM significantly outperformed state-of-the-art methods, localizing targets with 81% accuracy using only 4.5 pace-mapping sites compared to 38% using 13.7 sites.

Variational Consensus Monte Carlo for Bayesian Mixture

Presents a comprehensive pipeline for Bayesian mixture model inference in federated learning settings where data cannot be fully shared. It utilizes an extended Variational Consensus Monte Carlo (CMC) approach to estimate local posteriors independently across data silos and aggregate them. Key contributions include adapting variational CMC for mixtures that infer both the number of clusters and all parameters, even without assuming conjugacy. Off-Policy Evaluation for Missingness-Aware Policies in MDPs with Rewards Missing Not at Random

Investigates off-policy evaluation (OPE) in Markov decision processes when immediate rewards are Missing Not at Random (MNAR), which violates ignorability and causes selection bias. To address this, the work formalizes a reward-dependent propensity model utilizing future states as shadow variables to identify the full-data conditional mean reward. A novel bridge function is introduced to recover this conditional mean reward via a min-max procedure, avoiding explicit MNAR modeling. An accompanying Fitted-Q-Evaluation-style estimator propagates these recovered rewards and allows target policies to incorporate missingness indicators. The developed estimator demonstrates consistency and finite-sample error bounds, showing strong performance on simulated and real sepsis data.

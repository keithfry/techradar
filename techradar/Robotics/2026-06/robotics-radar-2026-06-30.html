ROS Discourse General: Guidelines for AI-Assisted Contributions in the ROS Project

Generative AI tools are permitted for developing and exploring ROS software, but contributions made on platforms like Discourse or GitHub must retain a human element. Contributors must fully own and defend every line of submitted code, as maintainers cannot act as validators for LLM output. Submissions containing hallucinations, such as non-existent APIs, will be immediately rejected. Furthermore, while disclosing the use of AI is required, PR descriptions must be concise, human-authored explanations of changes rather than generic AI text. Maintainers reserve the right to close submissions deemed low-effort, unverified LLM dumps or responses that fail to address technical critiques personally.

ROS Discourse General: Offboard Control with PX4 & ROS 2 (Micro XRCE-DDS)

Describes an architectural pattern for managing autonomous drone navigation in PX4's Offboard mode using ROS 2 (Jazzy) and Micro XRCE-DDS. A key challenge is that PX4 requires a continuous stream of setpoints at $>2\text{Hz}$ before accepting the transition to Offboard mode. The solution avoids blocking code, such as `std::this_thread::sleep_for()`, by using an rclcpp::Node wall timer. This timer drives a state machine, which first publishes dummy setpoints for one second to satisfy PX4's failsafe requirement before sending the actual Offboard and Arm commands. The resulting system maintains constant publishing at $20\text{Hz}$ without halting the ROS 2 executor thread.

ROS Discourse General: A Standard Interface from LLM to Robot Actions

Proposes a standardized interface for translating large language model (LLM) outputs into robotic actions across various platforms. The system defines 12 standard motion primitives, mapping natural language commands like "grasp" or "navigate_to" to specific ROS/2 topics and message types. This approach aims to solve the current problem where different robotics companies use inconsistent interfaces for the same tasks. Integration requires robots to adhere to publishing and subscribing on specified topics for components like arms, grippers, and base movement. Developers are seeking community feedback on the sufficiency of the 12 primitives and potential improvements over existing ROS/2 mechanisms.

A Query-Driven Communication-Efficient Digital Twins Design for Autonomous Driving

Digital twins offer risk-free simulation for autonomous driving but conventional methods incur high computing and communication costs due to constant data synchronization. This research proposes a query-driven DT architecture where the twin actively requests necessary environmental data based on its simulation outputs. An optimization problem is formulated to minimize positioning error while managing DT fidelity and strict communication limits. A progressive, cross-time-step query mechanism is introduced to further boost efficiency. Simulation results demonstrate that this approach reduces communication overhead by 40% and improves planning accuracy compared to traditional methods.

Event-Conditioned Diagnostics of Kinematic, Contact, and Object-Permanence Fields in Passive Object-State World Models

A controlled diagnostic protocol was developed to study how event-conditionings organize latent physical structure within passive object-state world models. Testing with free-motion, collision, and occlusion events, the research evaluated recurrent, attention-based, and state-space transition models. Results show that event contexts systematically reweight kinematic, contact, and object-permanence field readouts based on the current physical regime. Furthermore, causal field effect analysis demonstrated that suppressing specific field directions degrades event-relevant prediction, particularly for contacts during collisions. Findings support event-conditioned organization and functional sensitivity of these latent physical fields without requiring explicit, isolated physical modules.

When May I Help You? On The Effect of Proactivity on Group Human-Robot Collaboration

Robot initiative poses a significant challenge in human-robot collaboration because unprompted contributions can disrupt coordination, while waiting for direction might cause missed opportunities. Researchers tested this using a collaborative escape room with two models: reactive (responds only when addressed) and proactive (listens continuously and contributes autonomously). Results showed the proactive model increased interaction frequency, but the reactive model achieved a higher overall success rate in the study setup. Notable variations appeared based on user characteristics; those with prior LLM experience were faster in the reactive condition. Ultimately, robot initiative's impact is determined by a complex interplay of user experience, personality, and group needs.

Improvement of Robot's Simultaneous Localization and Mapping Using an Effective Transformation to Achieve Linear Model

Simultaneous localization and mapping (SLAM) is a critical task for mobile robots, often relying on Extended Kalman Filter (EKF)-based algorithms which suffer from divergence due to nonlinearities and linearization errors. This research addresses this limitation by applying an effective transformation using a simple compass. The transformation successfully converts the non-linear state space model into a linear one. By utilizing the standard Kalman Filter (KF) on this linearized model, a new method called LMKF SLAM is proposed. Experiments demonstrate that LMKF SLAM surpasses existing methods in accuracy, convergence, and computational efficiency while also exhibiting improved stability against sensor uncertainty.

FADA: Few-Shot Domain Adaptation via Dynamics Alignment for Humanoid Control

Humanoid control faces challenges from dynamics mismatch when operating in different environments or with payloads. To address this, FADA introduces a three-stage Planner-Inverse Dynamics Model (Planner-IDM) framework for few-shot adaptation. Initially, an oracle policy trains the system, and its behavior is distilled into the deployable IDM student using DAgger. At deployment, only the IDM is fine-tuned using minimal target-domain data—specifically, pairs of actions and observations from a brief rollout. This method aligns the action generation with the actual dynamics observed in the target domain, significantly outperforming existing adaptation techniques on real humanoid robots.

The Speedup Paradox: Rethinking Inference Speed-Quality Trade-off in Embodied Tasks

Embodied foundation models are increasingly used for robot generalization, often employing lossy efficiency techniques like quantization. Current efficient-inference studies inadequately account for the unique closed-loop effects inherent in repeated environmental interactions of embodied tasks. The paper proposes TISED, an analytical framework that unifies various inference optimization methods and decomposes their impact on static versus dynamic tasks. Research reveals paradoxical findings: optimization can sometimes increase overall completion time for static tasks while decreasing per-step latency. Furthermore, moderate lossy optimization may unexpectedly boost task success rates in dynamic settings, with these effects being sensitive to hardware configuration.

Embodiment Meets Environment: Toward Context-Aware, Safe Physical Caregiving Robots

Physical caregiving robots struggle due to systems being tightly coupled to specific environments and rigid robot designs. The proposed $E^2$-CARE framework addresses this by enabling context-aware adaptation for varied physical assistance. It achieves this using interaction templates representing primitive skills, which are reshaped online during execution. $E^2$-CARE models the environment, robot, and human within a unified 3D dynamic scene graph to explicitly capture interaction contexts. This allows safe, zero-shot reuse of skill templates across diverse settings and different robotic embodiments, validated in simulations and user studies.

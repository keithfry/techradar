RAG vs Fine-Tuning Explained: What They Actually Do and When to Use Each

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) and fine-tuning are distinct techniques for improving large language models, addressing different needs. Fine-tuning modifies the model's underlying weights using domain-specific data to change its style or knowledge base fundamentally. Conversely, RAG enhances responses by retrieving external, up-to-date information before generating an answer, grounding it in provided sources. The decision between them depends on whether the goal is behavioral adaptation (fine-tuning) or access to current/specific facts without retraining (RAG). They are complementary tools rather than competing solutions.

Interval Certifications for Multilayered Perceptrons via Lattice Traversal

A theoretical framework is presented connecting adversarial robustness to a lattice traversal problem concerning multilayered perceptrons (MLPs). A sound certification interval $I$ ensures that perturbing an input $\mathbf{x} \in I$ does not change the MLP's output, while a complete certification guarantees prediction change when moving outside $I$. The authors develop lattice traversal operators for a refine & verify iterative scheme, validating both sound maximality and complete minimality. They observe that finding minimum solutions for complete certifications is polynomial in oracle calls, unlike sound certifications which face strong intractability. Furthermore, logarithmic algorithms are provided for optimization problems within symmetric $\ell_\infty$-spheres, validated via the ParallelepipedoNN system.

This demonstrates robust analysis for nonconvex functional inequality constraints.

Stochastic Linear Bandits with Partially Observed Actions

Investigates partially observed stochastic linear bandits, where agents only receive random subsets of action coordinates. Since full observability makes sublinear regret impossible, the work focuses on cases with low intrinsic action dimension. A proposed algorithm, TOFU-POV, estimates the latent subspace from masked actions and runs OFUL in these lower dimensions. Theory proves TOFU-POV achieves $\sqrt{T}$ regret scaled by the subspace dimension, not the full ambient dimension. The research also introduces a rank-adaptive method and provides a novel lower bound demonstrating performance guarantees in this partial observation setting.

Optimal Top-$k$ Identification from Pairwise Comparisons

Investigates active learning for identifying the top-$k$ items using noisy pairwise comparisons until a specified error probability $\delta$ is met. The goal is to minimize the expected number of necessary comparisons (sample complexity). While asymptotic optimality exists for some pure exploration problems, an efficient algorithm remains unestablished for top-$k$ identification from these comparisons under latent utility models. Researchers address this by characterizing the lower bound as a saddle-point problem. This structure allows them to develop an online, primal-dual procedure and construct an adaptive algorithm proven to be asymptotically optimal.

AI 101: What Is a Token (and why it runs AI)?

A token is the fundamental unit into which text must be broken down before an AI model can process it. Models do not see words as humans do, but rather as sequences of these tokens, which are then converted into IDs and vectors for processing. Tokens are crucial because they dictate the model's capacity, response speed, memory usage, and operational cost, establishing them as the core currency of generative AI. A token is not always a whole word; it can be a part of a word, a punctuation mark, or a space. As a general estimate, one token is approximately four characters or about three-quarters of a word, though the precise count varies by language and tokenizer.

Understanding AI Tokens The Core Unit of Language Models

A token is the fundamental, discrete unit of text that an AI model processes and predicts, rather than entire words or sentences. Raw text must undergo tokenization to be converted into these model-readable tokens before processing can begin. These tokens are then converted into IDs and vectors for the model's attention mechanisms to use. Tokens determine critical parameters, including a model's context length, response speed, memory usage, and associated API costs. Because of these multifaceted implications, tokens function as the core technical and economic measure in modern AI systems.

Developing collision-free paths subject to geometric constraints remains challenging for complex robots, with current methods often taking excessive time. This work presents a novel CPU SIMD-accelerated manifold-constrained motion planner. The approach tackles constraint satisfaction by reimagining projection-based methods for massive parallelization. Utilizing SIMD parallelism transforms relevant components, leading to significant computational gains. This optimization achieves 100-1000x speed-ups, enabling real-time whole-body motion planning on hardware like humanoid robots.

Boundary Sampling to Learn Predictive Safety Filters via Pontryagin's Maximum Principle

Safety filters are crucial for enforcing constraints in autonomous systems. Learning-based tools require data covering states near constraint violations, which is hard to sample efficiently in high-dimensional spaces. This research uses the Pontryagin Maximum Principle to characterize trajectories that narrowly avoid safety limits. These "boundary trajectories" guide data collection for learning Hamilton-Jacobi Reachability, focusing training on critical states. Validation on automotive racing showed this PMP sampling method accelerates learning, reduces failures, and improves safe set reconstruction with fast computation times.

Singularity Avoidance in Inverse Kinematics: A Unified Treatment of Classical and Learning-based Methods

Singular configurations pose significant problems in inverse kinematics (IK) due to mobility loss and solver instability. This work offers a unified treatment covering classical techniques like Jacobian regularization alongside modern learning-based IK approaches. A systematic taxonomy is introduced to classify these methods based on geometric structure and robustness guarantees. By benchmarking twelve IK solvers on the Franka Panda, the study reveals that pure learning methods fail even under ideal conditions. Hybrid architectures, such as IKFlow and CycleIK, significantly improve learned solvers by integrating classical refinement mechanisms.

Robust Energy-Aware Routing for Air-Ground Cooperative Multi-UAV Delivery in Wind-Uncertain Environments

Aims to address the safety and reliability issues of UAV delivery in dynamic, partially observable wind conditions. Proposes Battery-Efficient Routing (BER), an online, risk-sensitive planning framework for truck-assisted aerial-ground logistics. This framework models the problem on a time-dependent energy graph whose costs account for wind-induced aerodynamic effects. BER continuously assesses return feasibility by balancing immediate energy use against inherent uncertainty. Simulations confirm that BER substantially improves mission success rates and reduces failures compared to static or greedy routing methods.

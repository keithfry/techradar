How JPL Keeps the 13-Year-Old Curiosity Rover Doing Science

Despite being thirteen years old and operating in Mars' hostile environment, the Curiosity rover continues scientific missions through careful software updates from JPL engineers. Significant longevity was achieved by swapping out failing computer components and ingeniously using redundant flight software memory as functional storage for one processor. Current constraints include wear on the wheels due to sharp rocks and decreasing power from its aging RTG nuclear source. To manage limitations, operations focus on increasing energy efficiency by cycling systems off when not needed. The experience guides future missions toward early operator input during design to better understand dynamic power management requirements.

Uncertainty-Aware Motion Planning for Autonomous Driving in Mixed Traffic Environment

Motion planning for autonomous vehicles in mixed traffic demands anticipating uncertain human driver behaviors. Current methods treating predicted intents as certain states risk unsafe decisions due to inherent uncertainty. We propose Uncertainty-Aware Motion Planning (UAMP), which explicitly incorporates intent uncertainty into decision-making. UAMP first estimates proximity-aware uncertainty and builds an uncertainty-guided joint intent distribution. By using Uncertainty-Calibrated Value Learning, UAMP corrects value function biases related to uncertain predictions, significantly improving safety and comfort in simulations.

arXiv - cs.RO

GHOST: Hierarchical Sub-Goal Policies for Generalizing Robot Manipulation

GHOST is a framework designed for learning visuomotor manipulation policies that can generalize beyond their training data. It decomposes control into two parts: a high-level policy predicting the next sub-goal as a 3D end-effector pose distribution, and a low-level goal-conditioned controller executing specific actions. A spatial interface is introduced to condition image-based policies on these 3D goals by projecting them onto heatmaps in the image plane. This hierarchical structure significantly improves performance and robustness compared to flat Diffusion Policies across various tasks. Furthermore, this design allows for easy integration of human demonstrations without requiring noisy action retargeting.

Robotic Nonprehensile Object Transportation with a Hanging Tray

Addresses the nonprehensile object transportation "waiter's problem" by using a real, rope-suspended hanging tray instead of rigidly tilting it. This setup allows the tray to behave like a three-dimensional pendulum while only actuating a 3 DOF mobile base, avoiding complex 6-DOF manipulator control. Experiments demonstrate that this dynamic swinging significantly reduces object sliding and liquid sloshing compared to static grasping methods. The system integrates an interactive demonstration utilizing computer vision to detect raised hands on people. Visual servoing is employed to direct the robot waiter toward the identified individuals for tray access.

arXiv - cs.RO

Efficient-WAM: A 1B-Parameter World-Action Model with Low-Cost Future Imagination

World-Action Models (WAMs) for embodied control are hindered by the high inference latency of photorealistic future visual prediction. Efficient-WAM addresses this by proposing a low-cost alternative that retains the benefits of future imagination. It achieves efficiency through a compact video expert, token-sparse video latents, and asymmetric video-action denoising. Instead of prioritizing visual fidelity, it uses future video prediction as a compact guidance signal for action generation. Testing on RoboTwin 2.0 shows that this 1B-parameter model maintains strong control while achieving a 30x speedup in latency down to around 100 ms.

arXiv - cs.RO

What Demonstration Curation Metrics Do to Your Policy

Research investigated whether metrics designed to detect defective training episodes improve subsequent behavior-cloning policies. Using a pick-and-place task with controlled structural defects, the study found that high defect-detection performance (AUROC of 0.804) resulted in a poor policy (13.3% success). Conversely, a metric with lower detection accuracy (AUROC of 0.638) yielded a near-oracle performing policy (90.0% success). The findings demonstrate that evaluation should focus on the resulting policy performance rather than solely the metrics used for defect flagging. Furthermore, the research cautioned that many current metrics improperly rely on episode length as a proxy for defects.

YUBI: Yielding Universal Bidigital Interface for Bimanual Dexterous Manipulation at Scale

Developed Yielding Universal Bidigital Interface (YUBI), a finger-aligned gripper addressing ergonomic limitations of bulky handheld data collection systems like UMI. YUBI utilizes yielding, finger-driven actuation directly mapping human finger movements to jaw motion for intuitive control. Using this system, researchers created an extensive dataset comprising 8,434 hours across numerous tasks. Testing demonstrated YUBI's superiority over the UMI gripper in dexterity and versatility for complex bimanual manipulation tasks. The integrated stack, including hardware, software, and data, allows policy trained on YUBI to transfer across various robot platforms.

What Matters in Orchestrating Robot Policies: A Systematic Study of Hierarchical VLA Agents

Hierarchical vision-language-action (Hi-VLA) systems use high-level language models to break down complex tasks into subgoals for low-level controllers, showing promise in robot manipulation. Current Hi-VLA designs lack unified principles regarding planner choice, controller connection, switching mechanisms, and observation representation. This paper systematically studies Hi-VLA design by unifying agents within an options-style control framework. The authors benchmark core design choices across varied tasks (short, long, reasoning) to distill actionable building principles. Applying these derived principles results in a significantly stronger system compared to flat or poorly designed hierarchical controls on both simulation and real hardware.

Locomotion analysis of a quadruped interacting with the lunar granular surface

Designing quadruped robots for lunar exploration requires considering motor torques, energy use, and cost of transport across challenging terrains. Lunar surfaces consist of granular regolith, which significantly affects robot locomotion. Locomotion algorithms assuming rigid contacts fail in soft, granular environments, leading to instability. Researchers modeled the physical interaction between a robot foot and granular regolith within a simulation. The analysis compared policies trained on rigid versus soft contact models, finding that simulating regolith increases energy expenditure and causes qualitatively different gaits.

Towards Data Science

Water Cooler Small Talk, Ep. 12: Byzantine Fault Tolerance

Byzantine Fault Tolerance, a system property that allows a system to continue operating properly even with malicious actors included in the system, has its roots in the Byzantine Generals Problem. This problem involves a group of generals who must reach a collective decision without knowing who among them is a traitor. In 1982, computer scientists Leslie Lamport, Robert Shostak, and Marshall Pease mathematically proved that for a system with n nodes to continue normal operation, at least n ≥ 3f + 1 total number of nodes is needed, where f represents the number of Byzantine (traitor) nodes. Blockchain technology has been found to be an elegant solution to this problem, specifically through its consensus mechanism called Proof of Work (PoW). PoW makes it computationally expensive for a traitor node to participate in the consensus process, rendering it economically irrational to cheat.

Rater State Bias in RLHF Preference Data: An Audit Framework

Rater State Bias in RLHF Preference Data: An Audit Framework proposes a structured confound in Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), where pairwise preference labels reflect not only the compared outputs but also the rater's state during annotation. Under stressful or distressing conditions, raters' preferences can shift over time, encoding rater state alongside judgments about response quality. This phenomenon, termed "rater state shift," is a plausible source of structured bias in RLHF preference data. The authors develop an audit framework to study this bias and propose five falsifiable predictions for initial audits.
